NETWORK ANALYSIS OF ANIME VIEWING BEHAVIOR
================================================================================

DESCRIPTION

This project analyzes the collective watching habits of hundreds of thousands
of anime viewers to construct and study a complex behavioral network. Rather
than relying on genre labels or critic scores, we build networks directly from
what users actually watch, complete, drop, and plan to watch — revealing
structure that reflects real taste communities, cultural bridges, and the
evolution of anime as a global medium. Using one of the largest publicly
available anime consumption datasets, we examine whether viewing behavior
recapitulates genre taxonomy or tells a richer story, which titles act as
structural hubs and cross-community bridges, and how the network grew and
changed as anime expanded from a niche medium into a worldwide phenomenon.

================================================================================
PROJECT DELIVERABLES
================================================================================

Deliverable 1 — Basic Network Construction & Structural Analysis :
Build the anime co-watch network where two anime are connected if a significant
number of users completed both. Characterize whether this network behaves like
a real-world complex network rather than a random graph. We plan on using
methods such as degree distribution, power-law exponent estimation, clustering
coefficient, average shortest path length, giant connected component analysis,
and comparison against random graph null models, among others.

Extra: Genre co watch network...

######################################################################################

Deliverable 2 — Bipartite Network & Multi-Layer Projection Analysis :
Construct the explicit bipartite user-anime graph, then build and compare
three separate co-watch projections based on different types of watching
behavior — completion, dropout, and wishlist — to understand whether anime
similarity changes depending on how users engage with a title. 

We plan on using methods such as bipartite degree distribution, bipartite clustering,
projection onto the anime layer, edge set similarity between the projected
networks, and identification of structural differences across the three
behavioral layers, among others.

######################################################################################

Deliverable 3 — Community Detection & Genre Alignment :
Apply community detection to the co-watch network and measure how well the
discovered behavioral communities align with the labeled genre taxonomy from
the metadata. Also build a genre co-occurrence network from metadata alone and
compare its structure to the behavioral communities. We plan on trying to
identify distinct viewer taste clusters, quantify how closely those clusters
match known genre groupings, find anime that act as bridges between otherwise
separate communities, and measure how pure or mixed each community is in terms
of genre, among others.

######################################################################################

Deliverable 4 — Centrality, Hub Characterization & Network Robustness :
Identify which anime are structurally critical to the network using multiple
centrality measures, locate cut vertices, and test whether the network is
robust to random failure but fragile to targeted hub removal. We also plan on
correlating centrality scores with node attributes to understand what properties
make an anime a hub. We plan on using methods such as degree centrality,
betweenness centrality, closeness centrality, PageRank, cut vertex
identification, targeted vs random attack simulation, and regression of
centrality against node attributes, among others.

######################################################################################

Deliverable 5 — Network Growth Analysis Over Time :
Build yearly cumulative snapshots of the co-watch network using the first-air
year of each anime and track how the network's topology evolved as the catalog
grew from the early 2000s to 2020. The goal is to identify whether a
percolation threshold exists — a point in time where the giant component
rapidly absorbed isolated clusters — and whether the network became more
scale-free as it grew. We plan on using methods such as yearly snapshot
construction, time series tracking of structural properties like giant component
fraction, average degree, clustering, and modularity, as well as degree
distribution comparison across early vs late snapshots, among others.
